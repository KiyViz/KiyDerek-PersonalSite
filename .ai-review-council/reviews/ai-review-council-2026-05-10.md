## Context Brief

**Project:** derekkiy.com — Derek Kiy's generalist freelance design practice website. Sister site to kiyviz.com (specialized infosplainers).

**Stage:** Planning complete, no code built. All specs, content inventory, and build instructions finalized.

**Business model (V-shaped):**
- Cylinder 1 (low-margin, high-volume): $97 paid consultations via Calendly+Stripe. 20-min discovery calls + 1-page executive hit list with automated scoring. Target: 1-2 calls/day.
- Cylinder 2 (high-touch, boutique): Done-for-you design services. Identity systems, reports, campaigns, museum content. Project-based pricing, 8-hr minimum on kiyviz.com side.

**Elevator pitch:** "Hi, I'm Derek Kiy and I make design systems. I like to work on creative projects that de-mystify knowledge and make it digestible. I produce plans, proposals, presentations, and production files for web, print, and physical spaces. I've contributed to museums, NGOs, foundations, campaigns, stores. I offer 1:1 design coaching and done-for-you design services."

**JTBD:** "Take design professionals & people from my network from curious yet skeptical to trusting and yearning by showing proof of work, creativity, and taste."

**CTA strategy:** One CTA above the fold — "Book a Call" -> Calendly ($97). No scroll needed. Portfolio below for trust-building.

**Key concern:** "I was told I'm 'good but expensive' — I need to change to 'worth every penny.'" Also: focus concern — "this freelance site seems to be the kitchen sink."

## 🎯 1. CEO

**Verdict:** Yellow-flag — the strategic bones are sound and the competitive moat is real, but the positioning narrative is scattered and the site risks being a "kitchen sink" that confirms the "good but expensive" perception rather than dismantling it.

**Strengths:**
- The V-shaped business model is genuinely clever. The $97 consultation cylinder is not just revenue — it is a repeatable, low-friction trust mechanism that converts network curiosity into paid relationships. A scalable word-of-mouth engine disguised as a product.
- The competitive moat is real and specific: bilingual EN/ES + cartography + museum + policy + editorial at sub-agency pricing. No one in that combination exists at this price point.
- The JTBD is sharp and honest: "curious yet skeptical to trusting and yearning." Naming skepticism as the starting state shows understanding of the actual conversion problem.

**Gaps:**
- The elevator pitch has seven sentences and four topic changes. If Derek can't land it in one sentence, the homepage can't land it in five seconds. "I make design systems" and "I produce plans, proposals, presentations..." are two different businesses.
- The "kitchen sink" fear is valid and unresolved. 10 case studies + photography + writing + now page + bilingual + 12 themes + two CTAs is accumulation, not curation.
- 0% of time on sales is a strategic emergency the website cannot fix. A website that automates trust-building is the right tool, but if no one distributes it, it generates zero pipeline.
- "Good but expensive" is a perception problem rooted in unclear value framing, not pricing. No outcome sentence exists for the $97 call.

**Priority Actions:**
1. Write a single-sentence positioning statement before any code is written. Format: "[Derek Kiy] helps [who] [specific outcome] by [distinctive method]." Every page traces back to this sentence.
2. Cut the hero to 7 curated cards, not 10+. Curation signals taste more than volume.
3. Define one distribution behavior for the next 90 days. One LinkedIn post/week, one direct outreach/day — something concrete.

**Score:** 6/10 — The market thesis is correct and the $97 cylinder is smart, but the narrative needs tightening to one sentence before execution begins.

## 🎨 4. Creative Director

**Verdict:** Yellow-flag — the creative direction is genuinely distinctive and the ambition is right, but two structural tensions need resolving before a single pixel is placed.

**Strengths:**
- The type stack is doing real work. Four typefaces, each with a locked role. Unbounded reads Mexican modernist without cosplaying it. Space Mono in captions says "I have a point of view about information." Not a Google Fonts grab-bag.
- "Con sabor y amor" as a scheme name is already brand language. Barragan + Legorreta + PRODUCTORA MX is a coherent reference constellation. The contrast with kiyviz.com's goblincore earth tones is smart sister-site differentiation.
- The polygraph.cool reading-flow reference is the right model. Bento grids flatter productized studios; ministories flatter range.
- All original imagery — no stock. Museum directors spend their lives looking at stock photography in grant proposals. One real bee photograph outperforms a hundred Unsplash assets.

**Gaps:**
- The controlled-chaos-vs.-institutional-trust tension is named but not resolved. What prevents the index-card hero from reading as a Notion screenshot? The chaos needs a visible organizing principle — a grid module, a card proportion, a consistent caption format — so disorder reads as curated, not accidental.
- The hand-drawn wordmark glyph is the highest-risk element. If it's confident — Saul Bass territory — it does enormous identity work. If it's hesitant or inconsistent across sizes, it signals effort without craft. No fallback is specified.
- 12 themes x "con sabor y amor" needs verification. KiyViz was built for earth tones. High-saturation Mexican palettes will stress every contrast differently.

**Priority Actions:**
1. Define card anatomy before writing CSS: physical proportion (3:5 index card ratio or break it intentionally), caption structure, the visual rule distinguishing "curated chaos" from noise.
2. Go/no-go on the hand-drawn glyph with a hard deadline. Commission it or commit to Unbounded with a programmatic underline.
3. Audit CSYA colors against three real content scenarios (dark-mode case study, contact section, 404) before writing final CSS.

**Score:** 7.5/10 — Creative direction is coherent and the references are earned; held back by two unresolved execution risks that could undermine the "real deal" signal.

## 🔧 5. CTO

**Verdict:** Green-light with yellow flags on font strategy and theme verification overhead — the architecture is well-matched to the actual scale.

**Strengths:**
- Stack proportionality is excellent. Vanilla HTML/CSS/JS for a personal practice site is correct. No bundler, no framework, no dependency graph to rot.
- Cloudflare Pages free tier is the right hosting choice. Git-push deploys, CDN, HTTPS, `_headers` for CSP. Zero devops burden.
- CSS custom properties + data attributes for theming is correct. JS enhances, CSS drives. Degrades gracefully.
- `content.js` as `window.DEREKKIY_CONTENT` is appropriate at this scale. Right data layer without requiring a CMS.
- Calendly + native Stripe = no custom payment code, no PCI scope.

**Gaps:**
- Google Fonts as primary with self-hosted fallback is backwards. Flip it: self-hosted primary, Google Fonts as enhancement or dropped. Direct conflict with LCP <= 1.5s target.
- 12 theme x WCAG AA verification has no enforcement mechanism. Manual checks will miss failures. Need automated contrast testing.
- Cache-busting via query string is unreliable for HTML files. Needs to be explicit about applying to asset references, not HTML URLs.
- Design token copy vs. symlink decision is open. Symlinks break on Cloudflare Pages. Copy with documented sync process is the answer.
- `window.DEREKKIY_CONTENT` with no JS-disabled fallback contradicts the "preferably readable" requirement. Cards should live in HTML with JS adding filter behavior.

**Priority Actions:**
1. Flip font loading: self-hosted unconditionally via `@font-face`, Google Fonts as preload enhancement or removed.
2. Decide the content.js / JS-disabled contract before building the hero. Cards in HTML, JS adds filtering.
3. Add a contrast verification script to the pre-deploy checklist for all 12 themes.

**Score:** 8/10 — Technically sound. Font strategy and JS/no-JS ambiguity are the only structural issues needing resolution.

## Executive Summary

**Overall Readiness:** Needs work — strong foundation, critical gaps in positioning narrative, traffic strategy, and content production.

**Consensus Strengths** (3+ reviewers agreed):
- The V-shape business model and $97 unit economics are genuinely sound (CEO, CFO, Marketing Director, PM)
- Portfolio depth and proof of work are legitimately competitive — NYT placement, museum work, trilingual materials are real credentials (Client Prospect, Creative Director, Marketing Director)
- Technical architecture is proportional, low-cost, and well-matched to scale (CTO, PM, CEO)

**Critical Gaps** (3+ reviewers agreed):
- No traffic or distribution strategy — the site is a conversion layer with nothing feeding it (CEO, CFO, Marketing Director)
- Derek's 12-17 hours of content production hasn't started and is the sole critical path item (PM, CFO, CEO)
- No testimonials or social proof near the CTA — the biggest trust gap for buyers (Client Prospect, Marketing Director, CFO)
- Above-fold hierarchy is unresolved — the CTA and portfolio grid compete for attention instead of serving distinct flows (Marketing Director, Client Prospect, CEO)

**The #1 Thing to Fix:**

Before any code is written, Derek needs to produce two things: (1) a single-sentence positioning statement that every page traces back to, and (2) an above-fold layout decision that gives the "Book a Call — $97" CTA visual dominance over the portfolio grid while keeping the grid as supporting proof. Without these, the build session will lock in strategic ambiguity and a broken Flow 1.

**Strategic Tension:**

The CEO and Marketing Director want fewer cards and a dominant CTA. The Creative Director and Client Prospect want the portfolio grid doing heavy visual lifting. Both are right for their flows. The resolution: a two-zone hero where the CTA + value prop occupies the left/top with clear dominance, and a curated card grid (5-7, not 10+) occupies the right/bottom as proof texture. The grid earns the click; the CTA captures it.
